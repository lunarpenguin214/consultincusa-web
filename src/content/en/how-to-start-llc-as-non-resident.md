## Why non-residents form US LLCs in 2026

Non-resident founders from India, Nigeria, Brazil, Mexico, the Philippines, Ukraine, and 50+ other countries are forming US LLCs at record numbers. The reasons are mostly the same:

1. **Accept payments in USD** from international clients. Stripe, PayPal, Wise, Mercury, and most B2B platforms work cleanest with a US entity.
2. **Sell on US marketplaces** — Amazon, Etsy, Shopify, Walmart often require a US business entity for certain categories or to avoid foreign-seller restrictions.
3. **Personal asset protection** — the LLC separates your personal name from business risks.
4. **Build US business credit** — opens doors to corporate cards, financing, vendor terms.
5. **Foundation for E-2 / L-1 visas** later if you ever want to relocate. The LLC alone doesn't grant a visa, but it's the prerequisite for several pathways.
6. **Credibility** — global clients treat US-incorporated businesses as more legitimate.

You don't need to live in the US. You don't need a visa. You don't even need to fly in.

## Can you really start a US LLC as a non-resident?

**Yes.** US federal law and every state's law allow non-citizens — including non-residents — to form LLCs. You do not need:

- US citizenship
- Green card or any visa
- SSN
- Physical presence in the US
- Fluent English

You **do** need:

1. To choose a state of formation
2. A **registered agent** with a physical address in that state
3. **Articles of Organization** filed with the state
4. An **EIN** (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS — the US tax ID
5. A **US business bank account** (the hardest step for non-residents)
6. To meet **annual compliance** obligations (annual reports, Form 5472, BOI / FinCEN, etc.)

Everything can be done remotely from your country in 7–14 days.

## Best state to form an LLC as a non-resident

You can pick any of the 50 states. For non-residents, three stand out:

### Wyoming — the default winner for non-residents
- **Filing fee:** $100
- **Annual report fee:** $60
- **State income tax:** none
- **Privacy:** high — owners are not listed in public records
- **Bureaucracy:** minimal
- **Best for:** digital businesses, e-commerce, freelancers, consultants operating from outside the US. The cheapest, simplest, most non-resident-friendly state for most cases.

### Delaware — the venture-capital state
- **Filing fee:** $110
- **Annual franchise tax:** $300+
- **Best for:** founders planning to raise from US venture capital firms. Nearly all VCs prefer Delaware C-Corps and LLCs because of the Court of Chancery (specialized business courts) and a deep body of corporate case law.
- **Avoid if:** you're running a simple e-commerce or services business and don't plan to raise VC. Wyoming costs less and gives you the same liability protection.

### New Mexico — the cheapest of all
- **Filing fee:** $50
- **Annual report fee:** **$0** (New Mexico does not require LLCs to file annual reports)
- **Privacy:** very high
- **Best for:** founders on a strict budget testing an idea
- **Caution:** some US banks treat New Mexico LLCs with extra scrutiny because of the privacy. Wyoming has the same privacy benefits without the banking-side friction.

### What about Florida, Texas, California?
Florida and Texas are popular for founders who plan to physically visit and open bank accounts in person. California has high franchise tax ($800/year minimum) and aggressive business taxation — usually a poor choice for non-residents unless you have a specific California physical-presence reason.

> **Recommendation for 9 out of 10 non-residents: Wyoming.** Cheapest, most private, simplest, and accepted by every US bank.

## The 14-day step-by-step plan

### Day 1 — Pick state and name
- Decide your state (Wyoming for most readers).
- Pick your LLC name. Must end in "LLC", "L.L.C.", or "Limited Liability Company". Cannot conflict with existing names in that state.
- Check name availability on the Secretary of State's website.

### Days 2–4 — File the LLC
- Complete and submit Articles of Organization to the state.
- Designate a **registered agent** with a physical address in that state.
- Pay state filing fee.
- The state issues your **Certificate of Formation** in 1–7 business days (Wyoming is typically fast).

### Days 4–8 — Operating Agreement and EIN
- Draft your **Operating Agreement**. Internal document defining ownership, management, and operating rules. Banks will ask for this.
- Apply for **EIN** by **fax or phone** (non-residents cannot use the IRS online application because it requires an SSN or ITIN).
- EIN issued in up to 4 business days by fax, or same-day by phone.

### Days 7–11 — Banking application
- Apply for a US business bank account: Mercury, Relay, Wise Business, or in-person at a traditional bank if you visit.
- Bank requests EIN, Articles of Organization, Operating Agreement, passport, and address verification.
- Approval typically 3–10 business days.

### Days 10–13 — Brand and operational setup
- Domain registration
- Business email
- Stripe and other payment processors
- Optional: 1-page launch site

### Day 14 — Open for business
- LLC formed
- EIN approved
- Bank account funded
- Stripe live
- You're operating.

## EIN without SSN: how to get one as a non-resident

The **EIN** is your business's tax ID. Without an EIN you cannot open a business bank account or file taxes.

Non-residents have **two paths** to get an EIN:

### 1. By phone — fastest path
- Call the IRS at **+1 (267) 941-1099** (this is a long-distance, non-toll-free international number).
- Hours: Monday–Friday, 6 a.m. to 11 p.m. Eastern Time.
- The agent will request your filled-out **Form SS-4**. You read the answers over the phone.
- You receive the EIN immediately, on the call.
- **Note:** the agent speaks English. Be prepared, or have a fluent speaker on the line.

### 2. By fax — slower but no live English required
- Complete **Form SS-4**.
- Fax to **+1 (855) 215-1627**.
- The IRS replies by fax within 4 business days with your EIN.
- Best for non-English-confident founders.

### When to apply for ITIN instead of (or alongside) EIN
- **EIN** = your business's tax ID. You always need this for the LLC.
- **ITIN** = your personal tax ID for individuals without SSN. You need an ITIN if:
  - You'll receive personally taxable US income (royalties, partnership distributions, etc.)
  - You need to file a personal US tax return as a non-resident with US-source income
  - Some banks (rarely) ask for an ITIN as part of beneficial-owner verification

ITIN application takes 7–11 weeks via Form W-7. A **Certifying Acceptance Agent (CAA)** can speed this up and removes the need to mail in your original passport.

## The Form 5472 trap that costs $25,000

If you're a non-resident sole owner of a US single-member LLC, the IRS requires **two annual filings:**

1. **Form 5472** — Information Return of a 25% Foreign-Owned U.S. Corporation
2. **Pro-forma Form 1120** — corporate income tax return (even if the LLC has no income or no US tax owed)

**Penalty for not filing: $25,000 per missed filing.** Per year. Per form.

## BOI / FinCEN reporting in 2026

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) requires most US-formed entities to file a **Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI)** report with FinCEN.

**2026 status:**
- US-created entities: **exempt** from BOI reporting per FinCEN's 2025 update
- Foreign reporting companies: **may still have BOI obligations** under the CTA — exact requirements depend on case specifics
- Most non-resident-owned single-member LLCs formed in the US are currently exempt, but rules are evolving

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I owe US taxes on my LLC profits as a non-resident?
**It depends.** If your LLC has no US-source income (no US clients, no US employees, no physical US presence, no inventory in US warehouses), generally no US federal income tax is owed — but you still file Form 5472 + pro-forma 1120. If you have US-source income, that income is subject to US tax. Your home country may also tax these earnings under controlled foreign corporation (CFC) or similar rules. Always work with a tax professional who specializes in cross-border non-resident LLCs.

### Can I use a US LLC alongside my home-country business?
Yes. Many non-residents use the US LLC as the international receiving arm to invoice non-domestic clients in USD, while keeping their home-country operating company for local clients and operations. Tax structure varies — get advice for your specific case.

### How fast is the LLC formation, really?
Wyoming: 1–5 business days for the state to issue the Certificate of Formation. EIN: same-day by phone or 4 business days by fax. Mercury: 3–7 business days for approval. **Total realistic timeline: 7 to 14 days.**

### Mercury rejected me. Can I try Stripe Atlas or Doola instead?
Mercury uses similar criteria across most resellers (Stripe Atlas, Firstbase, Doola all funnel to Mercury or Relay). If Mercury rejected you through one service, switching to another service usually doesn't change the outcome. The right move is a different bank (Relay, Wise) or in-person at a traditional bank.

### Does forming a US LLC give me a US visa?
**No.** The LLC is a business entity, not an immigration pathway. The LLC can be a foundation for visa applications later (E-2 investor, L-1 transfer) but only if you meet the visa's separate requirements. Always work with a US-licensed immigration attorney for visa work.

### What's the difference between an LLC and a C-Corp for non-residents?
- **LLC** = pass-through taxation by default (profits flow to owner), simpler, cheaper, more flexible. Better for most non-residents running solo, e-commerce, services, or consulting businesses.
- **C-Corp** = corporate-level taxation, required for most US venture capital, ESOP issuance to employees. Better if you plan to raise VC.

When in doubt, start with an LLC. You can convert to C-Corp later if needed.

### What if I want to dissolve the LLC later?
Dissolution is straightforward: file Articles of Dissolution with the state, file the final Form 5472 + pro-forma 1120, pay any owed taxes, close the bank account. Wyoming dissolution: $50 + filing fee. We handle dissolution at cost for our clients. **Do not** simply abandon the LLC — the state and IRS keep accruing fees and penalties.
