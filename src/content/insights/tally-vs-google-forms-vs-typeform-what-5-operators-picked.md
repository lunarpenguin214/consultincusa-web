# Tally vs Google Forms vs Typeform vs Zoho: what 5 operators picked when scale mattered

Nobody loves forms.

Every form on the internet was picked by a founder at 2am who had been putting it off for six months. They typed "best form tool 2024" into Google. They picked whatever showed up. They moved on with their life.

That decision compounds for years.

The form on your signup page is the first thing every potential customer touches. The form on your contact page is what determines whether 12% or 4% of curious visitors actually reach out. The form that captures your leads is the difference between "we tried marketing and it didn't work" and "we have a working funnel."

And yet — almost nobody audits their form choice once it's made.

This is the third installment in the receipt-reading series. [Read the philosophy piece](https://consultincusa.com/insights/53-percent-failing-businesses) and [the team chat comparison](https://consultincusa.com/insights/slack-vs-discord-vs-teams-what-5-operators-chose) if you missed them.

Let's look at what 5 named operators actually picked.

---

## The four contenders, briefly

**Tally** — Belgian indie SaaS. Free tier covers unlimited forms with no per-response cap. Paid plans $29-89/mo. Belongs to the "killer free product" generation.

**Typeform** — Spanish brand that built the conversational-form aesthetic 10 years ago. $25-99/mo for the plans most operators actually need. Polished but expensive.

**Google Forms** — free, ubiquitous, ugly. The form your daughter's soccer coach uses to collect snack assignments.

**Zoho Forms** — Indian SaaS conglomerate. $10-25/mo. Wins on compliance-driven enterprise mid-market. Nobody outside that segment chooses Zoho voluntarily.

Each tool serves a different operator. The operators below picked their tool deliberately — and the choice maps almost perfectly to their ambition and stage.

---

## Brand 1 — Nomad List (Pieter Levels): Tally

[Pieter Levels](https://levels.io) is the indie hacker prototype. Runs [Nomad List](https://nomadlist.com), [RemoteOK](https://remoteok.com), and several other businesses solo. Publicly reported revenue: $1M+ ARR per [his own dashboards](https://levels.io/projects/).

His form tool: Tally.

Receipts:

- Public tweets from 2023-2024 announcing his migration from Typeform to Tally
- Site embeds clearly visible — view source on any Levels.io property and search for `tally.so`
- He's vocal about pricing models — Tally won him over because no per-response charges scale infinitely with his traffic

**The lesson:** at $1M+ ARR running multiple SaaS products solo, "saving money on forms" isn't about budget. It's about hating per-unit pricing on something that should be flat.

**Sarcastic translation:** if you've made $1M+ as a solo founder and you still pay Typeform $50/month for forms nobody is judging the polish on — you're paying a brand tax. Typeform is selling you the IDEA that your forms need to look fancier. They don't. Function beats finish at indie scale.

## Brand 2 — Indie Hackers: Tally for community surveys

[Indie Hackers](https://indiehackers.com) — community + podcast for solo founders, now owned by Stripe. ~150K+ active members. Founder [Courtland Allen](https://twitter.com/csallen) is publicly active on Twitter.

When IH runs community surveys, post-event signups, or product feedback intake — Tally.

Receipts:

- Courtland has tweeted about Tally as the indie-hacker default
- View source on IH community pages — Tally embeds appear in event signup flows
- Tally aggressively courted the indie hacker market with their free plan; the IH community returned the favor by making Tally the in-crowd default

**The lesson:** when your audience IS indie founders, you use the tool indie founders use. Brand alignment. The IH community would clock you if you used a polished enterprise tool.

**For your business:** if your audience is operators, the tool you pick gets noticed. If you use Typeform's branded mode (their logo at the bottom of every form), you're advertising for them on your own page. Free with a watermark = you're paying with reputation.

---

## Brand 3 — Linear: custom-built forms (no off-the-shelf tool)

[Linear](https://linear.app) at ~$20-50M ARR doesn't use a form tool. They built theirs.

Receipts:

- View source on their [contact page](https://linear.app/contact) or signup flows — custom React components, not iframe embeds, no third-party domains
- Their support intake pages are built into the product UI itself, not third-party widgets
- Their public bug reports flow through GitHub Issues, not a form tool

**The lesson:** at $20M+ ARR with a design-led brand, off-the-shelf forms break your visual cohesion. You build forms in 4 hours that match your design system exactly.

The line operators miss: there's a moment around $5-10M ARR where you should consider building your own forms. Before that — off-the-shelf wins. After that — custom wins. Most operators stay on Typeform forever and pay $1,200/year for forms that don't match their brand.

**The 53% pattern:** trying to be "professional" too early (paying for Typeform at $200K revenue) or staying off-the-shelf too long (still on Typeform at $5M revenue). Match the tool to the stage.

## Brand 4 — Mercury: custom forms throughout

[Mercury](https://mercury.com) — banking for startups, $3B+ valuation per [public reporting](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/07/09/mercury-fintech-banking-startup-valuation.html). Their entire signup, KYC, application, and support intake flow is custom-built.

Receipts:

- View source on [mercury.com/start](https://mercury.com/start) — custom React, no form-tool embeds
- Even their support contact flow uses [Front](https://frontapp.com) (a help desk tool), not a form tool
- Their case study application flow is custom-rendered

**The lesson:** at $1B+ valuation with a regulated product (banking), every form is a compliance surface. You don't outsource that to a third party.

The signal for ambitious operators: when your tool choice would create regulatory or trust friction, custom-build. Banks don't use Typeform. Healthcare doesn't use Google Forms. Compliance-heavy industries build everything in-house.

**Translation for you:** ask yourself, "if I were Mercury, would I use Typeform for this form?" If the answer is "no, because trust matters here" — then your scrappy startup answer should be "Tally with a custom domain" not "Typeform with their watermark." Trust matters at every scale.

## Brand 5 — Beehiiv: custom signup form

[Beehiiv](https://beehiiv.com) — newsletter platform, $50M+ ARR. Their newsletter signup form (the most important conversion point on the site) is custom-rendered React.

Receipts:

- View source on [beehiiv.com](https://beehiiv.com) — signup form is hand-built, no third-party iframe
- The form is split-tested aggressively; you'll see different layouts in different sessions
- They monitor conversion rate to 4 decimal places — visible from analytics setup in their HTML

**The lesson:** when a single form IS the most important conversion event on your entire site, you don't trust a third party with it.

Beehiiv's bet: every basis point of signup conversion matters. A 0.3% improvement at their traffic scale is ~$50K/year in attributable revenue. Off-the-shelf form tools can't optimize for that level of detail. Custom can.

**For your business:** identify your single most important conversion form. Treat it with reverence. Custom-build it OR pick the tool that lets you optimize hardest (Tally has way more layout flexibility than Typeform at the free tier — surprisingly).

## The pattern, summarized

| Operator stage | Right tool | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Solo founder, $0-$1M revenue | **Tally** | Free tier covers your volume. No per-response gotcha. Embeds anywhere. |
| Brand-conscious B2B, $1M-$5M | **Typeform** (if you must) | Aesthetic premium for client-facing forms. Otherwise still Tally. |
| Internal-only, low-stakes forms | **Google Forms** | Free, ugly, fine for "let's pick a lunch place" |
| Compliance / Indian / enterprise mid-market | **Zoho Forms** | Specific buyer profile. Otherwise avoid. |
| $5M+ revenue, design-led brand | **Custom-built** | Off-the-shelf can't match your visual system. |
| $1B+ valuation, regulated industry | **Custom-built, no question** | Trust + compliance demand it. |

## The 53% pattern in this category

Operators in the failing 53% of small businesses pick forms wrong in three predictable ways:

**1. Paying Typeform $50-200/mo at $200K revenue.** The "professional brand" premium is psychological — your customers don't care about form aesthetics at your stage, they care about whether the form works. Tally does the same job for $0-29/mo.

**2. Using Google Forms for customer-facing intake.** The branded "Google Forms" header on a public-facing form tells customers you didn't care enough to pick a tool. It's worse than no form — it actively reduces trust.

**3. Never auditing their form's conversion rate.** Form conversion is the single most measurable funnel metric. 5% → 8% conversion doubles your inbound leads at zero ad cost. Most operators don't even know their conversion number.

The 47% above the line know their form conversion to the tenth of a percent. They split-test headlines. They reduce fields. They use Tally's "logic jumps" or Typeform's conditional questions to reduce drop-off.

**The 53% picks a form, embeds it, and never looks again.**

That's the pattern across every category I write about in this series. The tool isn't wrong. The thinking is. Or more precisely — the refusal to look at the result of the thinking.

## How to audit your form choice tonight

You don't need our help. You can do this in 20 minutes.

**1. View source on your own form.** Open Chrome, right-click → View Page Source. Search for `tally.so`, `typeform.com`, `docs.google.com/forms`, `zoho.com/forms`, or your custom domain. What's the form actually using? Surprise yourself.

**2. Find your form's conversion rate.** Use Google Analytics, Plausible, PostHog, or just calculate it: (form submissions ÷ form views) × 100. What is it? Anything under 8% is leaving money on the table.

**3. Count your form fields.** Each unnecessary field cuts conversion ~7%. If your contact form has 9 fields, you're losing ~50% vs. the same form with 3 fields. Brutal math.

**4. Check the form's mobile experience.** Open it on your phone. Try to fill it out one-handed. If you can't, neither can 67% of your traffic.

**5. View source on 3 competitor forms.** Same domain search trick. What are they using? Compare to your stack.

That's 20 minutes. That's the entire 53% vs 47% form decision.
