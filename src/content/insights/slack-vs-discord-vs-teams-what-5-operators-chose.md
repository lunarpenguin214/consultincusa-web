# Slack vs Discord vs Teams: what 5 named operators actually chose (and why your competitor isn't switching)

Every two months somebody on LinkedIn posts "We switched from Slack to Discord and our team is happier and we saved $400/mo."

The post gets 12,000 likes.

Then six months later, that same company is back on Slack.

You don't see THAT post. Nobody writes "We switched back."

So let me do you a favor. Instead of reading another self-congratulatory switching story, let's look at what 5 named operators — all running real businesses doing real revenue — actually picked, and why none of them are tweeting about switching.

This is part two of the receipt-reading series. [If you missed part one, start with the 53% piece.](https://consultincusa.com/insights/53-percent-failing-businesses)

---

## The four contenders, briefly

**Slack** — built for the work. Threads, integrations, search, channel architecture. Most expensive of the four ($7.25-$15/user/mo at Business+ tier).

**Discord** — built for community. Voice channels, server-roles, scales to thousands of members at $0 cost. Voice quality unrivaled.

**Microsoft Teams** — included if you already pay for Microsoft 365 Business. Effectively "free" if you're on the Microsoft stack. Bloated but enterprise-acceptable.

**Mattermost** — self-hosted, open-source. For teams that legally cannot put their data in someone else's cloud.

Each one wins a specific scenario. Each one loses badly outside that scenario. The operators below picked the right tool for THEIR specific scenario. Most operators don't even know what scenario they're in.

## Brand 1 — Linear: Slack (the obvious pick that's not obvious)

[Linear](https://linear.app) is the issue tracker that's eating Jira's lunch. Reported $20-50M+ ARR depending on the source. They run on Slack.

You can verify this in three minutes:

- Linear ships a [Slack integration](https://linear.app/integrations/slack) as a flagship feature — every Linear issue update pings the connected Slack channel. They built it because their users live in Slack. They use it because they're their own user
- Founder Karri Saarinen has tweeted from inside Slack screenshots multiple times
- Engineering job postings ([careers.linear.app](https://linear.app/careers)) consistently list Slack as a required communication tool

Here's the part most operators miss: Linear didn't "choose" Slack. **Slack chose Linear.** Once a company crosses ~$20M ARR with a developer-tools product, every customer integration request, every bug report, every cross-team coordination — happens in Slack. You don't get to opt out of that gravity.

**Sarcastic translation:** Linear's "tool choice" is what happens when you stop having a choice. That's not failure. That's product-market fit at scale.

## Brand 2 — Cal.com: Slack internal, Discord community (the right kind of split)

[Cal.com](https://cal.com) is open-source scheduling, post-Series A, ~$10M+ ARR depending on the source. Their team runs on Slack. Their community lives on [Discord](https://discord.gg/calcom) (~5,000+ members).

Receipts:

- Cal.com SELLS Cal.com for Slack as a productized integration. Public dogfooding
- Public Discord server is community-facing only — feature requests, support, contributor onboarding
- Founder Peer Richelsen has publicly discussed using Slack for internal ops on Twitter

**The lesson:** Slack for the team you pay. Discord for the audience you serve. Two different conversations. Cal.com knows the difference.

Most operators try to do both in one channel and end up doing neither. You'll see this pattern in agencies that put clients in their Slack workspace, or DTC brands that pull customers into Discord and then ask employees to handle support there. Mixed loyalty bleeds output.

## Brand 3 — Beehiiv: Slack internal, Discord community (same pattern, bigger numbers)

[Beehiiv](https://beehiiv.com) is the newsletter platform eating Substack's lunch (~$50M+ ARR per [public reporting](https://techcrunch.com/2024/06/06/beehiiv-newsletter-substack-tyler-denk/)). Public Discord server (~5,000+ members). Slack internally.

Receipts:

- Tyler Denk (founder) has tweeted from Slack screenshots multiple times — once specifically showing a feature decision thread
- Job postings reference Slack for engineering and growth roles
- The Discord server is creator-facing — newsletter operators using Beehiiv chat with each other there, share growth tactics, discover features

**Same pattern as Cal.com.** "We" lives in Slack. "They" lives in Discord. The split matters because if Beehiiv put customers in their Slack, every internal thread would derail into support. If they put their team in Discord, they'd lose threading, search, and integration depth.

The operators who confuse "we" with "they" are typically the ones running Discord for everything at $300K ARR, wondering why they can't find anything from last quarter.

## Brand 4 — Notion: Slack (the $10B company that doesn't need to be fancy)

[Notion](https://notion.so) is the productivity unicorn — last valued ~$10B, ~$300M ARR per [public reporting](https://www.reuters.com/technology/notion-raises-275-million-funding-round-2024-04-05/). They use Slack.

Receipts:

- Notion ships a [Slack integration](https://www.notion.so/integrations/slack)
- Engineering and product job postings list Slack as required tooling
- Multiple Notion VPs (Cristina Cordova, others) have referenced Slack workflows in public talks

**The lesson:** at $10B valuation and $300M ARR, you'd think a company building productivity software would build its own internal comms tool. They didn't. They use Slack. Because the cost of context-switching across thousands of cross-functional employees is more expensive than any tool license.

Translation for the rest of us: if Notion is on Slack, maybe stop pretending you're going to switch to Discord at $300K ARR to "save money." You're saving $7/user/month. You're losing time, integrations, and search. Bad trade.

## Brand 5 — Loom: Slack (the async-video company on synchronous chat)

[Loom](https://loom.com) is async video, acquired by Atlassian for $975M in 2023. Pre-acquisition stack (which mostly persists): Slack internally.

Receipts:

- Pre-acquisition job postings consistently listed Slack
- Engineering blog posts referenced Slack-based workflows
- Founder Joe Thomas has discussed team comms in podcast interviews

**Here's the irony.** Loom built an async video product because email and meetings suck. Their core thesis: most work doesn't need to be synchronous. And yet — they run on Slack, which is synchronous-by-default with async-by-thread.

Why? Because async video is FOR external communication. Internal team coordination still needs the threaded backbone Slack provides. Discord can do threads, but they're second-class citizens. Slack made threads first-class in 2017 and that single architectural choice locked in dominance for the next decade.

## The pattern, summarized

Five named operators. All five run Slack internally. Two of them ALSO run Discord — but only for public community, never for internal team ops.

This is the actual data, not the LinkedIn switching story.

| Scenario | Right tool | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Internal team comms, $1M-$1B+ revenue | **Slack** | Threads + integrations + search. Gravity. Period. |
| Public community for your product | **Discord** | Voice channels, server-roles, free at scale |
| Forced by Microsoft Enterprise contract | **Teams** | "Free" only because you're paying $36/user/mo for everything else |
| Privacy / compliance prevents cloud chat | **Mattermost** | Self-hosted. Government, security firms, healthcare |
| Mid-sized B2B operator who wants to "save money" by switching from Slack | **Stay on Slack** | The savings are real. The cost of disorganization is bigger. |

## What the 53% does wrong here

If you read [part one of this series](https://consultincusa.com/insights/53-percent-failing-businesses), you know the pattern. Operators in the bottom half of small-business income aren't dumb. They just don't look at the receipts.

In this category, the 53% pattern shows up as:

1. **Running Discord for internal team comms at $500K-$2M revenue.** Saves $100-300/month. Loses 200 hours/year in search-time, missed messages, and context loss. Math is brutal at any honest accounting
2. **Putting customers and team in the same Slack workspace.** Every internal discussion gets derailed by support tickets. Output halves. Nobody can speak freely
3. **Paying for Teams because Microsoft 365 came bundled, then using it half-heartedly while ALSO paying for Slack.** Two tools, half-use each, full cost. Operators do this constantly
4. **Picking Slack at $50K ARR and never customizing it.** Default channels, no integrations wired, no workflow automation. Same as not having Slack at all but you're paying $7/user/mo for the privilege

The pattern is always the same: the tool isn't wrong. The thinking is wrong. The 53% picked the right tool then refused to do the work that makes the tool earn its keep.

## How to read your competitor's comms stack in 5 minutes

You don't need to spy. The receipts are public. Here's where to look:

**1. Their integrations directory.** Most B2B SaaS publishes a /integrations or /partners page. Check if Slack is listed FIRST or featured prominently. That's a tell.

**2. Their job postings.** Search LinkedIn for "[their company name] Slack" or "[their company name] HubSpot." Required tools always appear in job descriptions. Last 5 hires = the actual current stack.

**3. Founder Twitter screenshots.** Most founders accidentally show their Slack interface in screenshots once or twice a year. Search "[founder name] Slack" on Twitter/X with image filter on.

**4. Their public Discord.** Type "site:discord.gg [company name]" into Google. If they have a public Discord, it'll appear. If it has 3,000+ members and active conversation, they're doing the Cal.com / Beehiiv split right.

**5. Their support tickets.** If their support email auto-replies show "Powered by Gorgias" or "Powered by Front" or "Powered by Zendesk" — that tells you their internal comms tool indirectly. Gorgias = usually Slack. Front = mixed. Zendesk = often Teams.

That's 5 minutes per competitor. Do this for 5 of your competitors. You'll know their comms stack better than they do.

## The takeaway

If you're under $1M revenue, the comms-tool choice doesn't matter that much yet. Pick Slack (or Discord if you're community-led from day one) and configure it well.

If you're over $1M revenue and still trying to "save money" by running internal ops in Discord, you're the 53%. Stop. Move to Slack. Configure integrations. Set up workflow automation. Your future self will thank you.
